Section 05
3-Day Window Hypothesis Analysis
Hypothesis
Solar electromagnetic events (CME, coronal hole HSS, M/X-class flares) produce geomagnetic disturbances that typically arrive at Earth 1–3 days after the solar event. The hypothesis is that formations appear preferentially within a 3-day post-event window following significant geomagnetic activity (G1+, Kp ≥ 5). The database already encodes solar_in_G1_window_3d, solar_in_G2_window_3d, and solar_in_G3_window_3d flags — these represent whether any G-level storm occurred within 3 days before or on the formation date.

Section 06
Solar Angle / Declination Analysis
Earth–Sun Angle Hypothesis
Solar declination varies from −23.4° (winter solstice) to +23.4° (summer solstice). UK crop circles cluster in summer months — but is this purely because crops are ready, or does the angle of solar radiation relative to Earth's surface play a role in the NoiseCore coupling mechanism? The database stores solar_declination per formation. A non-uniform distribution peaking at specific declination values — especially if it deviates from what the crop availability calendar alone predicts — would support a geometric coupling hypothesis.

Solar Angle Statistical Summary
Seasonal Confound
UK wheat grows May–August (declination +15° to +23.4° to +16°). UK OSR grows April–June (declination +5° to +23.4°). The majority of formations are in these crops. Without a control dataset of "days with crops present but no formation", it is impossible to distinguish solar angle preference from crop availability. To test the angle hypothesis rigorously, compare the declination distribution of formation-days against the declination distribution of all crop-present days in the same year range. This is a target for the next study phase.
